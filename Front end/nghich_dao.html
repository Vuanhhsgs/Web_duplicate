Inversion

Common Approach

When a problem involves many circles and becomes hard to handle directly, we can apply inversions with familiar properties to reduce it to an easier configuration, especially in cases involving tangency between circles or between lines and circles.

Example from a problem I solved

Illustrative Problem
Very Hard
(Source: Vietnamese Euclidean Geometry group on Facebook, user Duong Nguyen) Let triangle \(ABC\) be inscribed in circle \((O)\), with \(B, C\) fixed and \(A\) moving on the major arc \(BC\) of \((O)\). Let \((I)\) be the incircle of the triangle, tangent to \(BC\) at \(D\). Line \(AI\) meets \((O)\) again at \(P\). Let \((T)\) be the internal \(A\)-mixtilinear circle, tangent to \((O)\) at \(X\). Segment \(XP\) meets \(AD\) at \(L\).

a) Prove: \(L\) lies on \((T)\).

b) Let \(K\) be the reflection of \(A\) across line \(OI\). Let \(KD\) meet \((O)\) again at \(N\), and let \(NL\) meet \((O)\) again at \(J\). Prove: \(AJ\) always passes through a fixed point.

Try It Yourself

Solution:

Therefore, the problem is handled by using inversion centered at \(A\) with power \(AB\cdot AC\) combined with reflection about the bisector of \(\angle BAC\) to tackle the difficult configuration.
